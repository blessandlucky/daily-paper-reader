(Dated: May 12, 2026) Lithium electrolytes are commonly described using separate conceptual frameworks for local co-ordination chemistry, electrostatic screening, and ionic transport. This separation is effective in dilute conditions but breaks down at higher concentration, where coordination, ion pairing, clus-tering, and collective dynamics become intrinsically coupled. In this Perspective, we develop a unified multiscale framework that links local coordination motifs, mesoscopic ionic organization, and macroscopic transport within a single physical picture. Through representative examples span-ning carbonate liquids, polymer electrolytes, concentrated systems, and confinement, we show that increasing concentration drives a systematic evolution from solvent-dominated Li + coordination to ion pairing, clustering, and correlated domains. In this regime, screening and transport are not independent phenomena but arise from the same underlying correlated structures. This perspec-tive implies that rational electrolyte design must simultaneously control short-range coordination, mesoscale organization, and collective electrostatic response. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Electrolytes remain a central bottleneck in electro-chemical energy storage, as they simultaneously control ionic conductivity, interfacial chemistry, electrochemical stability, and power delivery [1, 2]. This is especially crit-ical in modern battery technologies [3, 4]. For lithium-based systems, the most natural microscopic starting point is the local coordination environment of Li +: the cation binds solvent molecules, polymer segments, or an-ions and forms a dressed [5] object embedded in a fluc-tuating charged medium. This viewpoint is chemically transparent and has been extremely productive, espe-cially for dilute liquid electrolytes. That picture is nevertheless incomplete once one moves away from the dilute limit. Concentrated elec-trolytes, solvent-in-salt systems, polymer electrolytes, ionic-liquid-based formulations, and hybrid materials all exhibit strong correlations, pronounced deviations from ideal transport, and screening behavior that cannot be reduced to isolated solvation shells [6–10]. As the salt concentration increases, anions enter the first coordina-tion shell, ion pairing and clustering become important, and extended ionic domains may emerge. The relevant physical objects are then no longer bare ions, or even merely solvated ions, but much more complex correlated ionic structures, whose identity depends on both local chemistry and collective organization. The objective of this Perspective is to develop a uni-fied multiscale framework that connects these levels of de-scription. The conceptual development of this framework is illustrated in Fig. 1. The central claim is that coor-dination, screening, and transport should not be treated as separate topics, but as different manifestations of the same underlying hierarchy of correlated structures. Lo-cal coordination determines the elementary motifs from which ion pairs, clusters, and larger ionic domains are built. Those larger structures, in turn, control dielectric response, screening, and the effective carriers relevant for charge transport. The discussion is organized around representative ex-amples, largely drawn from our own work where appro-priate. Consequently, it should by no means be regarded as an exhaustive survey of the extensive literature in the field. We also emphasize, however, that the perspective developed here builds upon concepts and insights that are already present in the literature, albeit often in a frag-mented or dispersed form. Most of the data and illustra-tions we consider originate from molecular simulations, which provide a consistent and controlled framework for analysis. This emphasis reflects the fact that experimen-tal probes, while essential, are often not sufficiently se-lective to unambiguously isolate the most relevant mi-croscopic degrees of freedom and their correlations. By contrast, simulations allow one to resolve shell compo-sition, cluster statistics, and transport correlations at a level of detail that helps disentangle the underlying phys-ical mechanisms, while the resulting physical picture is intended to remain broader than any single methodology. We begin by examining local coordination in molecu-lar and polymer electrolytes, and then introduce a hier-archy of length scales that links short-range structure to mesoscopic organization and electrostatic screening. We next address how confinement perturbs this hierarchy, and finally show how a unified description of transport and design principles emerges from this perspective. If established, such a framework could guide the rational design of electrolyte materials by explicitly connecting molecular-level structure to macroscopic transport prop-erties. II. LOCAL COORDINATION AS THE ELEMENTARY STRUCTURAL PROBLEM A. Carbonate solvents and the tetrahedral motif 

For conventional carbonate electrolytes, the classical starting point is the interaction of Li + with carbonyl oxygen atoms. Ethylene- (EC), propylene- (PC), and dimethyl-(DMC) carbonates differ significantly in molec-ular shape, dielectric character, and flexibility, yet they all offer strong donor sites to Li +. A broad body of simu-lation and quantum-chemical work indicates that, at low concentration, Li + typically adopts a fourfold first shell coordination in these media, with approximate tetrahe-dral local order [6, 11–16], as illustrated in Fig. 2. This picture is chemically natural: the small cation favors strong short-range interactions with a limited number of donor oxygens, and four-fold coordination is consistent with both steric and electrostatic constraints. At the same time, the tetrahedral motif should not be over-interpreted as a rigid molecular complex. The simulations of [16] make an important point: even in di-lute carbonate mixtures, the local environment of Li +

is embedded in substantial structural heterogeneity ex-tending to distances more than five times larger than the first coordination shell radius. This is already a first sign that the electrolyte cannot be reduced to a collection of isolated clusters. The shell is local, but its composition and dipolar environment are influenced by fluctuations on larger scales. This point matters because the literature on carbonate solvation has long been marked by apparently conflict-ing coordination numbers extracted from Nuclear Mag-netic Resonance, Raman, mass spectrometry, and simula-3tions [17–22]. Part of the discrepancy is methodological, but part is physical: the ”coordination number” is not al-ways a sharply defined observable. It depends on concen-tration, on the operational cutoff used, on whether one counts molecules or donor atoms, and on whether anions participate in the same shell. What survives across meth-ods is the robust importance of a short-range carbonyl-oxygen cage around Li +, and the exceptional sensitivity of this cage to composition and concentration.  

> B. Preferential solvation in mixed solvents

Mixed solvents provide a clear demonstration that lo-cal coordination is not controlled by polarity alone. If electrostatics were the only criterion, the most polar cyclic carbonates would always dominate the first shell. Yet both simulation and electronic-structure work show a more subtle picture. For EC/DMC and EC/DMC/PC mixtures, DFT calculations on isolated clusters tend to favor more strongly polar components, whereas molecu-lar dynamics in the liquid may predict enhanced DMC participation in the first coordination shell [6, 7, 16, 20, 22, 23]. This is not a contradiction; it reflects the fact that local structure in the liquid is shaped by packing 

and by collective dipolar cancellation , not only by iso-lated binding energies. The simulations of Ref. [16] are especially illuminating on this point. Within a locally tetrahedral shell, the vec-tor sum of the solvent dipoles can be strongly reduced when similar molecules occupy the shell in symmetric ar-rangements. Under such circumstances, the total shell dipole may actually increase when a less polar linear car-bonate contributes, because it disrupts otherwise more complete dipole cancellation. This is a remarkably im-portant lesson for electrolyte design. The question is not simply which solvent binds Li + more strongly, but which mixture produces the most favorable balance of local ge-ometry, shell dipole, and bulk transport.

> III. POLYMER COORDINATION AND THE EXTENSION OF THE SAME IDEAS TO SOLIDS

Polymer electrolytes are frequently discussed as a sepa-rate class of electrolytes, but many of the structural ideas discussed above apply. The same coordination principles hold, but their consequences become inherently dynami-cal, as ion transport is now coupled to the slow relaxation of the host matrix [28–30]. The major difference is that the donor atoms belong to a deformable, slowly relaxing matrix rather than to a molecular solvent. As a result, coordination and transport become strongly entangled with the internal dynamics of the host. In poly(ethylene oxide)-based electrolytes, Li + is sta-bilized by ether oxygens, and its migration is tied to polymer segmental motion [28, 29]. In carbonate-based polymer electrolytes, such as LiPF 6/polypropylene car-bonate, the donor groups are more directly reminiscent of liquid carbonate solvents, but the dynamical landscape is much slower and more heterogeneous. The recent simu-lations of [30] show that such systems exhibit strong ionic correlations, only a limited fraction of truly free ions, and a large population of negatively charged clusters at the highest salt concentrations. Even when the local Li +–carbonate interaction is not very strong on a pairwise basis, the network of ionic and polymeric constraints can suppress the mobility of the cation more than that of the anion at experimentally relevant temperatures. This, again, reinforces the same message: local coordi-nation is the elementary structural problem, but not the whole story. The polymer matrix changes the statistics of site availability, residence times, and exchange pathways. What appears as a simple coordination shell in a static structural snapshot is, dynamically, a transient and con-strained network. That network determines whether Li +

moves by carrying a coordination shell, by exchanging among neighboring coordination sites, or by participat-ing in the motion of larger ionic aggregates. Hybrid polymer/ceramic systems offer another instruc-tive example. The recent analysis of SiO 2 nanoparticles in PEO/LiTFSI shows that adding passive nanoparti-cles can reduce, rather than enhance, ionic conductivity above the melting temperature of the polymer [31]. The reason is not merely geometric obstruction, important as it is, but a reorganization of local coordination and species distributions into distinct regimes separated by a concentration threshold. In other words, filler addition changes the phase space of local coordination environ-ments and, therefore, the statistics of transport path-ways. The fact that this can be understood with the same coordination language used in liquids is conceptu-ally important. Finally, liquid-crystalline solid electrolytes provide an intermediate case in which ion-conducting domains be-come structurally ordered on nano-scales while still allow-ing local Li + hopping [32]. These systems make explicit what is otherwise hidden in conventional electrolytes: lo-cal coordination motifs can self-organize into mesoscopic channels, and transport then depends on the architecture of the coordinated domains as much as on the shell itself.  

> IV. A HIERARCHY OF LENGTH SCALES

Building on previous work on ion pairing, clustering, and mesoscale ionic organization [10, 33–36], the argu-ments developed so far point to a simple but important conclusion: electrolyte structure cannot be described at a single scale. Instead, it is useful to introduce a hierar-chy of organization levels that bridge local coordination and macroscopic response. A schematic version of this hierarchy is shown in Fig. 1, where the relevant objects evolve from first-shell coordination motifs to ion pairs, clusters, and finally correlated domains associated with screening and collective transport. At the smallest scale, the basic object is a motif : the local coordination environment of Li +, including solvent molecules and, at higher concentration, anions. This scale is set primarily by short-range chemistry—ionic ra-dius, donor strength, steric shape, and solvent or poly-mer flexibility. In carbonate liquids it corresponds to the carbonyl-oxygen cage around Li + and is typically of or-der a few ˚ A [16, 23, 24]. In polymers, the same scale is defined by coordinating ether oxygens, nitriles, or other donor groups embedded in a slower and more constrained matrix. The next level is the cluster scale. Here local motifs are no longer independent, but assemble into contact ion pairs, solvent-separated ion pairs, and larger associated ionic aggregates. This is the first scale at which local coordination and concentration meet directly: whether the anion remains outside the first shell or enters it de-fines a genuine structural crossover. Cluster analysis in battery electrolytes has shown that even chemically sim-ple mixtures can generate rich populations of associated ionic species [33–36]. In polymer electrolytes these as-sociated structures may become long-lived and dominate the charge balance of the ionic sub-ensemble [30, 33–36]. At still larger distances one reaches correlated domains .These need not be permanent phases; they may instead be dynamically evolving regions over which charge corre-lations, mesoscale order, or partial network connectivity persist. In liquid-crystalline electrolytes, for example, ordered ionic domains and lamellae explicitly introduce this scale [32]. More generally, the correlated domain is the natural level at which one should discuss screening and collective transport, because it is this organization— rather than isolated ions—that determines how charge fluctuations are transmitted and relaxed. This hierarchical description is useful because it uni-fies phenomena that are often treated separately. Local 5coordination determines the elementary charged motifs; those motifs assemble into clusters; and clusters or larger correlated domains control macroscopic observables such as screening lengths, conductivity, transference, and the validity or failure of Nernst–Einstein-like descriptions. In that sense, the hierarchy shown in Fig. 1 is not a mere list of unrelated scales but a coarse-graining sequence linking chemistry, structure, and collective dynamics [10, 34–36].   

> V. SCREENING: FROM DEBYE TO CORRELATED IONIC DOMAINS A. Why Debye–H¨ uckel becomes insufficient

The Debye–H¨ uckel picture remains the proper asymp-totic starting point for low ionic strength [37, 38]. In that limit, ions behave approximately as weakly coupled point charges embedded in a dielectric continuum, and the interaction between charges is screened over the De-bye length. But battery electrolytes are usually far from that regime. The local dielectric response is heteroge-neous, ion sizes are not negligible, molecular shapes mat-ter, and ion pairing or clustering can be substantial. Here, we focus specifically on two closely interrelated questions within the modern screening literature. First, how should one describe the crossover from monotonic Debye-like decay to oscillatory strong-coupling behav-ior [39]? Second, what is the physical meaning of the unexpectedly large screening lengths (dubbed as under-screening ), further increasing with salt concentration, inferred from surface force balance [40] experiments in concentrated electrolytes and ionic liquids [5, 39, 41– 45]? The terminology of regular versus anomalous un-derscreening has become standard, but the interpretation remains actively debated [46–48].  

> B. A cluster-based interpretation of screening

The simulation study of Ref. [10] provides a particu-larly useful framework because it directly links structural, dielectric, and transport data in a single model system over a huge concentration range. Several points from that work are especially important for the perspective developed here. First, the electrolyte does not simply become “more correlated” in a vague sense as concentration increases. Rather, the ionic organization evolves through identifi-able structural regimes, including the growth, aggrega-tion, and eventual percolation of ionic clusters [10, 33–36]. Second, the correlation length relevant for screening can be interpreted more naturally when one assigns effective charges to correlated ionic domains instead of insisting on bare-ion charges. Third, the largest deviation between the true conduc-tivity and the Nernst–Einstein estimate occurs where electrostatic interactions are strongest and where the cluster-based description becomes essential. In this sense, the effective charge carrier undergoes a progres-sive renormalization with increasing concentration: from a solvated ion defined by local coordination, to a par-tially neutralized ion pair, and ultimately to correlated ionic clusters that act as the relevant objects for both transport and screening [8–10, 34, 36]. This is a conceptually attractive result because it closes the loop with local coordination. If contact ion pairing and shell neutralization change the identity of the mov-ing charge carrier, then they should also change the ef-fective object that participates in screening. The same elementary redefinition of the carrier propagates upward in scale. Viewed in this way, screening is not an abstract continuum property layered on top of chemistry; it is the long-wavelength consequence of the chemistry hav-ing already reorganized the charged fluid into dressed, clustered entities.  

> C. Screening as a design parameter

From a practical point of view, the relevance of screen-ing goes beyond surface force balance experiments. It determines the electrostatic coupling between charged species, influences dielectric friction, constrains ionic mo-tion, and affects how electrolytes respond to confinement or interfaces. Because these properties are concentration dependent and structurally renormalized, electrolyte de-sign should include not only first-shell binding arguments but also control over the growth of correlated ionic do-mains. This idea is already implicit in several classes of elec-trolyte engineering. High-concentration formulations de-liberately trade off some free-ion mobility for interfacial benefits and altered solvation chemistry [27, 49]. Single-ion or anchored-anion systems aim to restrict the an-ion contribution to conductivity and reshape the effec-tive screening environment [50]. Liquid-crystalline elec-trolytes attempt to impose mesoscale order on ionic path-ways [32]. In each case, screening serves as a unifying physical diagnostic of whether the targeted local coordi-nation motifs translate into the intended collective orga-nization.  

> VI. ELECTROLYTES UNDER CONFINEMENT

From this perspective, nanoconfinement is not a separate prob-lem from bulk electrolyte physics, but a controlled per-turbation of the same hierarchy of coordination, clus-tering, and screening scales [10, 31, 49]. > VII. A UNIFIED PICTURE OF LITHIUM TRANSPORT

Within the framework developed above, transport is not an independent phenomenon layered on top of struc-ture and screening. It is the dynamical consequence of the same correlated objects that define local coordina-tion, ion pairing, cluster formation, and screening re-sponse. Fig. 4 summarizes three limiting transport mech-anisms.  

> A. Vehicular motion

The first limiting mechanism is vehicular transport: Li + moves together with a relatively persistent coordina-tion shell. This picture is most natural in dilute carbon-ate electrolytes, where the cation behaves as a dressed particle defined by its local solvation environment [16]. It remains useful whenever solvent exchange is slow com-pared with translational motion, or when the shell iden-tity is sufficiently long-lived that the cation effectively carries its local environment with it. Vehicular motion is nevertheless not equivalent to ideal independent-ion diffusion. The mobility depends on shell size, solvent viscosity, dielectric friction, and the dipolar structure of the coordination environment. Preferential solvation therefore affects not only thermodynamics but also dynamics: two shells with similar mean coordination number can exhibit different mobility if they differ in symmetry, dipole cancellation, or solvent friction.  

> B. Structural exchange and hopping

The second limiting mechanism is structural diffusion, in which Li + transport proceeds through exchanges be-tween coordination environments rather than by persis-tent motion of a single intact shell. This mechanism is especially important in polymer electrolytes, where the cation migrates between transient coordination sites de-fined by polymer segments [28–30], but analogous ex-change processes can also occur in structured liquids and liquid-crystalline systems [32]. In this regime, transport is controlled by the competi-tion between residence times and exchange rates. If coor-dination is too strong, Li + becomes trapped; if too weak, dissociation and structural connectivity may be lost. Fast transport is therefore often associated with intermediate coordination strength, where local binding remains suf-ficient to maintain a connected ionic environment while allowing rapid rearrangement between neighboring mo-tifs. 8 

> C. Collective cluster motion

The third limiting mechanism is collective transport. In concentrated liquids and polymers, charge may be car-ried not only by nominally individual ions but by asso-ciated ionic entities or correlated domains. This gener-ally reduces the accuracy of independent-particle trans-port pictures and is one important origin of large devi-ations from the Nernst–Einstein relation [8–10, 30]. De-pending on cluster charge and lifetime, collective motion may either reduce net conductivity through anticorre-lated cation–anion motion or open new transport path-ways within connected ionic morphologies. This mechanism is the natural dynamical counterpart of the cluster-based view of screening developed in the previous section. If correlated domains provide the phys-ically relevant screened objects in static correlations, they should also influence current relaxation, conductivity, and transference. In that sense, Nernst–Einstein failure is not merely a breakdown of a formula; it is a signature that the wrong effective carriers have been chosen. The same considerations apply to the lithium transfer-ence number, which cannot be interpreted as an intrin-sic single-ion property in correlated electrolytes. As ion pairing, clustering, and collective dynamics develop, the effective charge carriers become correlated entities rather than individual ions [8, 9, 56]. The measured transfer-ence number then reflects the net contribution of these collective modes, including anticorrelated cation–anion motion, and may become small or even negative despite significant lithium mobility [8, 56]. In this framework, the transference number emerges as a direct dynamical probe of the correlated structures that govern both screening and transport.  

> D. One framework, three weights

Vehicular, hopping, and collective mechanisms are therefore best viewed not as separate theories, but as three concentration-dependent weights in a common transport framework. Their relative importance depends on solvent composition, polymer relaxation, salt identity, and concentration. Dilute liquids are usually dominated by vehicular transport; intermediate concentrations show increasing shell exchange and ion pairing; highly concen-trated liquids and many polymer systems require explicit cluster and network language. The central point is that transport evolves continuously with the same correlated structures that also control screening and mesoscale or-ganization. A key implication of this framework is that trans-port does not change through discrete mechanisms, but through a continuous redistribution of weights governed by the stability and dynamics of correlated structures. In particular, optimal transport often emerges in regimes where coordination is sufficiently robust to sustain con-nectivity, yet sufficiently labile to allow rapid reorganiza-tion. At higher concentration, where correlations extend over multiple length scales, transport becomes increas-ingly sensitive to the topology and lifetime of ionic aggre-gates and domains, and single-particle descriptions lose predictive power. In this regime, quantities such as the lithium transference number and the notion of single-ion transport must be interpreted in terms of these correlated carriers rather than individual ions. From a design perspective, this implies that improv-ing electrolyte performance requires controlling not only local coordination chemistry, but also the emergent mesoscale organization and collective dynamics it in-duces. We return to this point in more specific terms in the next section.  

> VIII. A MULTISCALE COMPUTATIONAL FRAMEWORK FOR CORRELATED ELECTROLYTES

The hierarchy of length scales discussed above provides a unified picture of electrolyte structure. A natural next step is to translate this picture into a predictive compu-tational framework (see Fig. 5) capable of propagating in-formation from the nanoscale, where chemical specificity resides, to the macroscale, where transport and screening are experimentally probed. This is not simply a problem of bridging spatial and temporal scales, but of identifying the relevant degrees of freedom at each level of descrip-tion and ensuring consistent information transfer across qualitatively different representations. 

a. Electronic-structure level: defining coordination motifs. Electronic-structure calculations provide the most fundamental description of the electrolyte, by re-solving ion–solvent and ion–ion interactions at quantum-mechanical scales [6, 7, 57]. Beyond binding ener-gies, they provide information on polarization response, charge distribution, and the free-energy landscape asso-ciated with different coordination environments. Within the present framework, these calculations define the el-ementary coordination motifs that form the microscopic building blocks of the electrolyte. Importantly, the rel-evant information extends beyond isolated clusters: lo-cal dipoles, anisotropy, and environment response must also be characterized, as they influence assembly at larger scales. 

b. Atomistic simulations: statistical realization of motifs and clusters. Classical Molecular Dynamics sim-ulations extend this description by sampling the statisti-cal ensemble of coordination environments, cluster pop-ulations, and dynamical processes [10, 16]. This level provides direct access to coordination-number distribu-tions, ion-pairing statistics, cluster lifetimes, and current correlations entering transport coefficients. At this scale, motifs become dynamically interacting entities that assemble into clusters and, at higher concen-tration, extended correlated domains. c. Coarse-grained representations and emergent car-riers. A central step in the multiscale framework is the definition of coarse-grained variables that capture intermediate- and large-scale behavior. In the present context, these variables naturally correspond to clusters and correlated ionic domains that govern screening and transport. This requires identifying relevant structural objects through cluster analysis or connectivity crite-ria [33–35], and assigning them effective properties such as charge, mobility, and interaction potentials. This mapping is not unique: effective charge depends on inter-nal correlations and polarization, while dynamical prop-erties depend on both internal structure and coupling to the surrounding medium. Preserving the physical con-tent of this mapping is therefore essential. 10 

d. Machine learning as a unifying layer. Machine learning (ML) provides a powerful framework for both constructing interatomic models and identifying coarse-grained representations. At the atomistic level, machine-learned interatomic potentials enable simulations that retain near ab-initio accuracy while reaching system sizes and time scales inaccessible to direct electronic-structure methods [58, 59]. This is particularly impor-tant for electrolytes, where polarization and many-body effects are central. More fundamentally, ML offers a systematic route to identifying collective variables gov-erning information transfer across scales. Unsupervised and representation-learning methods can extract low-dimensional descriptors of local environments, cluster configurations, and network connectivity directly from simulation data [59, 60]. A particularly promising direction is the use of graph-based representations, where ions and solvent molecules are treated as nodes connected by interaction-dependent edges. Within this framework, clusters and correlated domains emerge naturally as subgraphs whose proper-ties can be learned and propagated across scales [61–63]. Such approaches offer a direct route to linking local coor-dination patterns with macroscopic observables such as conductivity and screening length. 

e. Interfaces between scales: consistency and infor-mation transfer. A critical aspect of any multiscale framework is the treatment of couplings between dif-ferent levels of description. These couplings are best viewed as mappings between distinct representations of the same physical system. Three mappings are partic-ularly important. First, electronic-structure calculations provide information on local energetics, polarization, and charge transfer that must be translated into classical force fields or machine-learned interatomic potentials for large-scale simulations [64, 65]. Second, atomistic simula-tions must be connected to coarse-grained descriptions in which molecular configurations are projected onto clus-ters or correlated domains [60, 66]. Third, coarse-grained models must be linked to continuum descriptions, where transport, screening, and electrochemical response are defined at experimental scales. Consistency across these mappings requires preserving key observables under coarse-graining, including thermo-dynamic quantities, structural correlations, and trans-port coefficients. Reduced models must therefore re-produce both static structure and dynamical observables such as current autocorrelation functions, collective diffu-sion coefficients, and dielectric relaxation. ML provides a natural framework for enforcing such consistency by learning mappings constrained simultaneously by multi-ple observables [59]. 

f. From correlated structures to macroscopic re-sponse. Within this framework, information propagates through a sequence of transformations linking micro-scopic structure to macroscopic response. Local coor-dination determines elementary charged species, cluster formation modifies their effective charge and mobility, and mesoscale correlations shape collective transport. Quantities such as screening length, conductivity, and transference number therefore emerge from this sequence rather than constituting independent inputs. The corre-lation length associated with ionic domains provides a natural extension of the Debye length beyond the dilute limit, while deviations from Nernst–Einstein behavior re-flect the growing importance of correlated transport. Several challenges remain in realizing this framework in practice. A first concerns the accurate treatment of polarization and many-body effects across scales, which are essential for quantitative electrolyte modeling [67]. A second is the identification of transferable coarse-grained variables that remain robust across chemistries and ther-modynamic conditions [68]. A third is the treatment of rare events and long-time dynamics, which are often cen-tral to transport yet difficult to sample directly [69]. Despite these challenges, the combination of electronic-structure methods, large-scale simulations, and machine learning offers a promising route toward predictive mul-tiscale modeling of electrolytes [70]. By connecting co-ordination chemistry, mesoscale organization, and collec-tive dynamics within a unified framework, this approach could transform electrolyte design from a largely empir-ical process into a predictive discipline.  

> IX. WHAT THIS IMPLIES FOR ELECTROLYTE DESIGN

The unified picture developed here has a direct design implication: local coordination cannot be optimized in isolation. A solvent, polymer architecture, or additive is not beneficial simply because it binds Li + strongly or weakly. It is beneficial only if the resulting coordi-nation statistics, cluster populations, and collective re-sponse produce the desired transport, screening, and in-terfacial behavior. This principle suggests several con-crete heuristics: 

a. Do not optimize coordination number alone. 

Four-fold coordination may be a useful dilute-limit refer-ence, but under practical concentration the more relevant descriptor may be the full distribution of solvent-plus-anion donor motifs [23–27]. 

b. Track shell dipole and local symmetry, not just shell composition. Mixed-carbonate results show that less polar components can still favor transport-relevant local structures through packing effects and dipolar can-cellation [6, 7, 16]. 

c. Treat ion pairing as a structural transition, not just a chemical equilibrium. When anions enter the first shell, the effective carrier changes identity. This directly affects conductivity, transference, and screen-ing [23, 24, 27]. 

d. Interrogate cluster statistics whenever Nernst– Einstein fails strongly. Large deviations often indicate that the relevant moving objects are correlated domains rather than nominally independent ions [8–10, 34, 36]. 11 

e. Use mesoscale organization strategically. Liquid-crystalline and hybrid-electrolyte examples show that morphology can either enable connected ionic pathways or suppress transport, depending on how local coordina-tion reorganizes around interfaces and domains [31, 32]. Taken together, these points suggest that rational elec-trolyte design should target the coupled evolution of local coordination, mesoscale ionic organization, and collective dynamics. The practical goal is not to optimize a sin-gle descriptor, but to engineer a hierarchy of structures whose combined effect is favorable for both transport and electrochemical stability.  

> X. OPEN QUESTIONS AND BROADER APPLICABILITY ACROSS ELECTROLYTE SYSTEMS

Several important questions remain open. A first con-cerns the relation between screening lengths inferred from surface measurements and those extracted from bulk cor-relation functions. The cluster-based interpretation of Ref. [10] is physically consistent, but it still needs to be tested across a wider chemical range and under confine-ment; related cluster and network perspectives point in the same direction [34–36]. A second question pertains to the coupling between coordination dynamics and interfacial reactivity. The same shell motifs that control transport also influence the chemistry from which interphases emerge [2, 49]. A predictive theory of electrolyte performance should there-fore connect shell statistics, screening, and decomposition pathways rather than treating them as separate prob-lems. A third open issue is methodological. Electronic-structure calculations remain essential for donor prefer-ences and local motifs, whereas classical molecular dy-namics is needed to resolve larger-scale correlations and collective transport. Bridging these levels, especially when polarization and many-body effects matter, re-mains a central challenge [7, 57, 71]. A final open issue concerns the generality of our per-spective, specifically its validity across a broader range of materials. Indeed, although the present discussion has focused primarily on lithium-based electrolytes, the con-ceptual framework developed here is expected to apply broadly across a wide class of ionic liquids and battery chemistries [1, 2]. In particular, sodium-, potassium-, magnesium-, and zinc-based electrolytes exhibit many of the same structural motifs observed in lithium systems, including solvent coordination shells, ion pairing, and the formation of transient ionic aggregates [72–74]. The relative importance of these features, however, depends strongly on ion size, charge density, and solvent–ion in-teraction strength. Sodium-ion electrolytes provide a particularly impor-tant point of comparison. Because Na + ions possess a larger ionic radius and lower charge density than Li +, their coordination environments are typically more weakly bound and dynamically more labile. As a result, sodium electrolytes often exhibit weaker ion pairing and larger coordination numbers, which may significantly in-fluence the balance between vehicular and hopping trans-port mechanisms [72, 73]. Similar trends are expected to emerge in potassium systems, where the larger ionic ra-dius further reduces electrostatic binding and may favor more solvent-separated ionic structures. Multivalent electrolytes introduce additional complex-ity. In magnesium or zinc systems, the stronger Coulomb interactions associated with higher ionic charge can pro-duce more tightly bound solvation shells and stronger ion–anion correlations [74]. These effects may promote cluster formation and correlated ion motion even at rela-tively low salt concentrations, potentially leading to sig-nificant deviations from dilute-solution transport mod-els. Understanding how coordination chemistry and elec-trostatic correlations interplay in such systems remains an important challenge for the development of next-generation battery electrolytes. Finally, the view we have discussed here points to the need for improved coarse-grained descriptors for materi-als discovery. If the key variables are not merely dielec-tric constant and donor number, but shell dipole distri-butions, cluster charge statistics, and correlation lengths, then new descriptor sets will be required for both physics-based screening and data-driven design.  

> XI. CONCLUSIONS

The traditional separation between coordination chem-istry, screening theory, and transport theory is increas-ingly unhelpful for modern lithium electrolytes. These systems are better understood as multiscale charged flu-ids in which local coordination motifs, ion pairs, clus-ters, and correlated domains form a connected hierarchy. Within that hierarchy, screening and transport emerge not from isolated bare ions, but from the statistics and dynamics of correlated ionic structures. The physical picture may be summarized simply. At low concentration, Li + is often described reasonably well as a cation dressed by a local solvent shell. With increas-ing concentration, anion participation modifies that shell and partially neutralizes the dressed ion. The resulting motifs assemble into ion pairs, clusters, and sometimes network-like ionic domains. Those correlated objects, rather than nominally isolated ions, increasingly deter-mine conductivity, transference, dielectric response, and apparent screening lengths. The practical implication is equally direct. Electrolyte design should target the full hierarchy of structure shown in Fig. 1, not only the first shell. Improving performance requires joint control of coordination chemistry, the onset of ion pairing, the growth of clusters, and the collective screening landscape that emerges from them. This is a more demanding program than local optimization alone, 12 but it is also a more realistic basis for rational design. Future work combining molecular simulations, ad-vanced scattering techniques, and theoretical modeling will be essential to quantitatively connect these struc-tural correlations with macroscopic transport properties. Such efforts may ultimately enable the rational design of electrolytes in which coordination environments, screen-ing length scales, and correlated transport pathways are tuned to optimize conductivity and electrochemical sta-bility across different battery chemistries.
