(Dated: July 13, 2026) When a micron-sized bubble bursts, capillary waves deform the cavity into a cone that ejects a Worthington jet. The jet is born by inertial focusing, and the local collapse follows self-similar Euler solutions set by the semiangle β. Writing rj and vj for the dimensionless jet-base radius and velocity, the local Weber number W e j = rj v2 

> j

measures inertia relative to capillarity. The theory, supported by accurate numerical simulations gives rj ∝ τ α(β) with α ≃ 0.63 and, hence 

W e j ≫ 1, with W e j → ∞ as rj → 0, so inertia increasingly overwhelms capillarity. In simulations, the interface collapses onto a universal shape for more than two decades in dimensionless time when lengths are scaled using our prediction for rj . For water, this gives incipient radii of O(1) nm, predicting nanometric sea-spray aerosols. 

Bubble bursting ejects Worthington jets whose breakup transfers material across liquid–gas interfaces, from sea spray [1–8] and rain-induced aerosols [9] to sparkling wines [10] and electrochemical sprays [11]. The jet dynamics and minimum drop sizes are set by capillary-wave focusing [12–17]. The radii R of the bub-bles for which submicrometric droplets can be produced via bubble bursting jets in a surfactant-free or negli-gibly contaminated interface of a liquid of density ρ,dynamic viscosity μ and interfacial tension coefficient 

σ, is characterized by a value of the Ohnesorge num-ber Oh = μ/ √ρRσ ≈ 0.03 [13, 15]; for water, this means micron-sized bubbles with Bo = ρgR 2/σ ≪ 1, with g indicating gravity [18]. In this regime, capillary waves deform the bubble into a conical cavity of semi-angle β and entrap a tiny bubble beneath the cavity be-fore the fastest, thinnest jet is emitted [19, 20] (Figs. 1 and 2(a)(i)–(iv)). The local question is what similarity law governs the focusing flow after this conical cavity has formed. Far from being an academic problem, a precise description of the physical laws that govern the jet’s for-mation is essential to accurately predict the size of the resulting ejected drop which, eventually, will turn into the aerosol particle. In the classical inertio-capillary interpretation, dy-namic and capillary pressures balance during collapse, giving rj ∝ τ 2/3 and a local Weber number W e j =

Conclusion. —We have derived a family of self-similar solutions of the incompressible Euler equations for the collapse of a conical gas cavity of semiangle β. The jet radius and velocity scale as rj (τ ) ∝ τ 1/(2 −ν(β)) and 

vj (τ ) ∝ τ (ν(β)−1) /(2 −ν(β)) , with ν(β) the degree of the Legendre function satisfying Pν (− cos β) = 0. For the measured bubble-entrapment range 35 ◦ <

In the self-similar re-gion, W e j (τ ) ∝ τ (2 ν−1) /(2 −ν) diverges as τ → 0, and the DNS gives both W e j ≫ 1 and a two-decade collapse of the free surface near the jet base when lengths are scaled with rj ∝ τ 1/(2 −ν[β(Oh )]) . Thus Oh controls access to the singularity by selecting β, the inception time, and the regularisation scale; once the similarity window exists, the local collapse is governed by inertia. Matching this inertial similarity to the viscous cutoff gives incipient radii Rm ∼ O (1) nm for water bubbles with R ∼ 15 μm, suggesting that bubble bursting can produce nanometric aerosol precursors to cloud conden-sation nuclei and ice-nucleating particles. We solve the axisymmetric two–phase incompressible Navier–Stokes equations with the free software Basilisk C [32–34]. We fix ρg /ρ l = 10 −3 and μg /μ l =2 × 10 −2; all results reported in the Letter use Bo = 0 and Oh ≃ 0.03, unless stated otherwise. For Bo = 0, the initial condition is a spherical bubble of radius R intersecting a flat free surface. The point con-tact is regularised by a capillary bridge of size δ = 0 .01 R,leaving an initial opening of radius ≃ 2δ. The computa-tional domain spans −6 ≤ z/R ≤ 4 and 0 ≤ r/R ≤ 10 for the production cases; the lower axial boundary is a no-slip, no-penetration wall, the upper axial boundary is an outflow, and r = 0 is the axis of symmetry. Adaptive mesh refinement is applied to the volume fraction, velocity, and curvature fields. To resolve the focusing singularity at jet inception, we developed a feature-tracking refinement criterion that follows the largest incoming curvature wave before inception and the newly formed jet thereafter. Data used in Figs. 2 and 3 use M AXlevel = 15, corresponding to a finest cell size ∆/R = 10 /215 ≃ 3.1 × 10 −4.Grid independence is checked in Fig. 2 by repeating the Oh ≃ 0.03 case at M AXlevel = 13, 14, and 15. The results for focus levels 13–15 collapse onto the same scaling over the fitted asymptotic window. The plotted scalings use the M AXlevel = 15 data, resolve the jet base down to rj ≃ 5 × 10 −3, and fit the asymptotic window 0 .005 ≤ rj ≤ 0.024. The jet radius is the outer free-surface base returned by a tag-based probe that excludes detached droplets and entrained gas satellites; the base fluxes are integrated across the same axial plane (see Fig. 2).
