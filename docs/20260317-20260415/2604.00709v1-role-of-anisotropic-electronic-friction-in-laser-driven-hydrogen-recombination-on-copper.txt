(Dated: April 2, 2026) Ultrafast light-driven chemical dynamics at surfaces are governed by energy transfer from excited electrons to vibrational degrees of freedom. When this nonadiabatic energy transfer is anisotropic, it can lead to dynamical steering effects that affect reaction probabilities or non-thermal final energy distributions in molecules. Here, we use a machine-learning-enabled simulation framework to com-pare isotropic and anisotropic models of electronic friction during laser-driven hydrogen evolution on the (111) facet of copper. While anisotropic friction strongly determines the rate of energy transfer into the adsorbate and the fluence dependence of reaction probabilities, it has little effect on final translational, vibrational and rotational energy distributions as these are mainly governed by the potential energy landscape at the barrier. 

Adsorption and desorption of molecular hydrogen from metal surfaces are important steps in heterogeneous catalysis and electrochemical processes [1]. Although these steps are thoroughly studied in thermally activated catalysis, many open questions remain for photocatalytic processes on metal interfaces [2]. When a metal surface or nanoparticle is exposed to light, electron-hole pair (EHP) excitations are generated efficiently [3]. These non-thermal excitations rapidly thermalise into a ”hot” electron distribution through electron-electron scattering with an effective temperature above the surrounding lat-tice [4]. Equilibration with the lattice through electron-phonon scattering occurs at a picosecond scale during which chemical dynamics at the surface can occur [4, 5]. As a result, hot electrons can transfer energy into ad-sorbed atoms or molecules at a faster rate than into the lattice, driving chemical transformations [6] such as the recombinative desorption of H 2 from Ru(0001) [7]. Laser-desorbed H 2 molecules generally have a non-thermal partitioning of energy across vibrational, ro-tational and translational degrees of freedom [6]. It is still not clear if this non-equipartition of energy in the molecule results from non-thermal heating of chemisorbed hydrogen or from details of the barrier land-scape during recombinative desorption. Ultrafast pump-probe experiments report a transient change to adsorbate vibrational modes, e.g. the red-shift of the CO stretch vibration frequency on a Cu(100) substrate, [8] which has been interpreted as a consequence of non-thermal and mode selective EHP-mediated energy transfer into the adsorbate. Laser-induced dynamical effects can also enhance isotope effects [9], promote trapping in precur-sor states [10], or induce direction-specific diffusion [11]. The vibrational anisotropy of the coupling between hot electrons and adsorbate vibrations has been shown to affect dynamics in measured and calculated vibrational linewidths [12–14] of adsorbed hydrogen, scattering of atomic hydrogen [15], and dissociative chemisorption of H2 [16, 17]. Classical molecular dynamics simulations impose the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and are unsuitable to describe non-adiabatic electron-nuclear coupling ef-fects, as they neglect the Born-Oppenheimer approxima-tion [18]. While a fully ab-initio description of electron dynamics triggered by a laser pulse is possible [19], simu-lations of the ensuing coupled-electron nuclear dynamics of realistic interfaces are still computationally challeng-ing [20]. A commonly employed mixed quantum-classical approach to study light-driven dynamics at surfaces is the Molecular Dynamics with Electronic Friction (MDEF) method [21–24]. In addition, a study by Lindner et al on laser-driven H 2 evolution from Ru(0001) [9] recently showed how finite size effects alter desorption yields, calling the accuracy of low-dimensional surrogate models into ques-tion. Virtually all light-driven dynamics simulations so far have evaluated the electronic friction tensor (EFT) via the isotropic Local Density Friction Approximation (LDFA), where friction coefficients are calculated from ion scattering shifts in a homogeneous electron gas that matches the electron density of the substrate at the position of the adsorbate atom [29]. While the non-equipartition of energy in desorbed molecules has been successfully predicted for H 2 evolution on Ru(0001) [22], previously predicted laser fluence dependences do not agree with experiment [6, 9]. In this Letter, we perform full-dimensional TTM-MDEF simulations of laser-driven H 2 evolution from a Cu(111) surface using a tensorial and anisotropic de-scription of orbital-dependent friction (ODF) that is directly calculated from first-principles electron-phonon coupling [30–33]. ODF has been shown to accurately re-produce the mode dependence of vibrational lifetimes [15, 32] and spatially anisotropic energy dissipation effects in reactive scattering of N 2 on Ru(0001) [34] and H 2

on Cu [35]. H2/Cu(111) exhibits a highly activated late barrier (0.592 eV desorption barrier, fig. 1c) and strong anisotropy of friction across cartesian coordinates of chemisorbed hydrogen and internal modes of molec-ular hydrogen during recombination (fig. 1b). We com-pare desorption probabilities, surface diffusion behaviour and energy partitioning for isotropic friction as predicted by LDFA and anisotropic, mode-dependent friction as predicted by ODF. We find that the topology of the PES dominates the final energy distributions of desorbed molecules, but the two different EFT models differ sig-nificantly in the on-surface diffusion that they induce, yielding drastically different fluence dependence. The re-sults indicate that the mechanism of H 2 recombination is primarily affected by the shape of the PES, while the rate of recombination and fluence dependence is determined by the level of theoretical description of non-adiabatic coupling.

3The TTM-MDEF simulations are enabled by previ-ously reported high-dimensional machine learning mod-els of the PES and the EFT [17, 36, 37] trained on accurate Density Functional Theory data based on the SRP48 functional [38]. As shown by a detailed valida-tion against DFT data (reported in the Supporting Ma-terial [39]) [17, 21, 22, 29, 32, 33, 36, 37, 40–52], the models extend to the description of light-driven recom-binative desorption, enabling the efficient simulation of several thousands of trajectories for each model and laser fluence condition. The TTM-MDEF simulations include surface degrees of freedom and the effect of laser-heating on the phonons. After energy input from the laser pulse (fig. 2a), the elec-tronic temperature T el rises rapidly to over 5000 K within approximately 0.3 ps, while the phononic temperature Tph rises slowly. The rapid increase of T el leads to ac-tivation of hydrogen atom diffusion already within the time span of the laser [9]. The rate of kinetic energy transfer is determined by the magnitude of the EFT. On average, adsorbed hydrogen atoms gain kinetic en-ergy roughly twice as quickly in LDFA simulations com-pared to ODF (fig. 2b). The increased kinetic energy of the adsorbed hydrogen atoms raises the frequency of collisions between adsorbates, which in turn can lead to recombinative desorption if enough energy has been absorbed to cross the recombination barrier. As a re-sult, the rate of desorption (fig. 2c) reaches a maximum shortly after the maximum kinetic energy transfer into the hydrogen atoms is reached (about 0.5 ps for LDFA, 0.75 ps for ODF). While the desorption rate decreases more rapidly for LDFA simulations, the distribution of desorption events over time is broader and decays more slowly for ODF simulations, indicating a slower relax-ation after laser excitation. To analyse the diffusion behaviour of adsorbed hy-drogen atoms, we partition the primitive unit cell of the Cu(111) surface into areas associated with high-symmetry sites which are commonly used for the dis-cussion of reaction energetics in ab-initio studies [53] (fig. 3a). For a subset of about 200 trajectories per elec-tronic friction approximation, we follow the motion of the adsorbed hydrogen atoms, measuring the number of transitions between sites. Based on the different site-to-site hopping rates, the diffusion rate of hydrogen atoms is roughly 20% slower on average in ODF simulations as a result of the weaker energy transfer (fig. 3b). The site hopping rate in LDFA simulations reaches a higher peak value, but decays slightly faster than observed in ODF simulations. We also compare the population of the different surface areas by H atoms over time for LDFA and ODF (fig. 3c-d). For both friction approximations, hydrogen atoms initially reside in the hollow sites, the global energy minimum, and begin to populate higher energy bridge and top sites after laser excitation. The mode-selective ODF based on a first-principles de-scription with DFT response theory has proven to af-fect reaction dynamics of some adsorbates on metal sur-faces [8]. For example, in quantum-state resolved scat-tering [54] H 2 molecules formed through laser-driven des-orption are known to desorb translationally hot, and with preference for certain rotational and vibrational states, however it is unclear whether the topology of the PES or the energy transfer due to electronic friction (or both) realise this behaviour [6, 55]. Analysis of the translational, vibrational and rota-tional energy of desorbed molecules reveal subtle dif-ferences in the non-thermal energy distributions of molecules (fig. 4b). In general, desorbing H 2 molecules in ODF simulations have less translational, vibrational, and rotational energy than in LDFA simulations. However, the absolute vibrational energy remains nearly as high as its LDFA counterpart. This is the result of stronger non-adiabatic coupling along the bond axis (fig. 1b) [17, 56]. It is likely that the total vibrational energy is slightly overestimated in both sets of simulations due to the semi-classical Einstein-Brillouin-Keller [51] analysis and rounding to integer quantum states [22]. Due to the high symmetry and lack of steps or edges on the Cu(111) sur-face, collision events between hydrogen atoms are most likely to occur when both atoms are at similar heights over the surface, and the MEP favours a concerted mo-tion away from the surface. These two factors likely dis-courage cartwheel-like rotations on desorption, leading to desorption with, on average, low rotation energy. This is particularly so for ODF where the component of friction perpendicular to the surface is significantly lower than in LDFA. Since the choice of electronic friction approximation has a large impact on desorption probability, but a com-paratively small effect on energy distribution in desorbing H2, we look to the time scales and energetic impact of electronic friction within our simulations. In fig. 5, the effect of different terms within eq. (1) on the dynamics is shown in the form of an overall energy contribution along a representative ODF trajectory. The state of H 2 formation is classified by the H-H dis-tance, which decreases to the equilibrium bond distance at approx. 0.75 ˚ A during recombination, with some os-cillation caused by vibration. After diffusion around lo-cal energetic minima, a collision with sufficient energy to cross the barrier to desorption (fig. 1c) leads to motion away from the surface, indicating the onset of desorp-tion. The potential energy term ∇V fluctuates based on the positions of the hydrogen atoms during the trajec-tory, with average values remaining similar throughout the simulation. From the beginning of the trajectory until the onset of desorption (fig. 5a), the thermal fluc-tuation term dW transfers approximately 120 meV into the hydrogen atoms. The high electronic temperature, combined with a higher magnitude of electronic friction experienced by hydrogen atoms close to the surface results in a contin-uous overall energy uptake. Λ ˙R, the velocity-dependent friction term, dissipates energy out of the system, but at a slower rate compared to the random force term that de-scribes the energy uptake due to the elevated electronic temperature. However, during the tens of femtoseconds before and after the onset of desorption (fig. 5b), the en-ergy transfer contributions due to thermal fluctuations and friction are less significant than those by the poten-tial energy surface term, as the atom velocities are com-paratively low at the recombination barrier. While there is some evidence of pre-organisation of the two hydrogen atoms prior to desorption (at approximately t = 300 fs), we did not observe this for a majority of trajectories. Based on similar observations for LDFA and ODF des-orption trajectories, we can conclude that the ultrafast laser pulse drives reactivity through photothermal acti-vation of hydrogen diffusion over several hundred fem-toseconds. Instead, the desorption is mainly governed by the shape of the PES. These conclusions are consistent with observations from theoretical simulations and experiments on H 2 scatter-ing. For example, Spiering et al [56] and Stark et al 

[17] determined that frictional coupling along the vibra-tional axis of H 2 is stronger for ODF compared to LDFA, however, the choice of LDFA compared to ODF did not significantly affect sticking probabilities. Since scatter-ing H 2 molecules are only close to the surface for a short time, EFT values, even though they might be strongly anisotropic along different modes, do not strongly affect the dynamics, despite significantly affecting the final en-ergy distributions. Prior to dissociation, in the diffusion regime, where the distance between H atoms is large, off-diagonal ele-ments of the ODF EFT are significantly lower than diag-onal components, so differences between LDFA and ODF mostly depend on the magnitude of diagonal EFT com-ponents. ODF coefficients are significantly lower than LDFA perpendicular to the surface, resulting in lower energy gain (fig. 2) and reduced efficiency in mobilising hydrogen atoms at the surface (fig. 3b). However, at higher surface coverages (here presented simulations were performed at 2/9 ML coverage), the shorter average H-H distances during diffusion lead to increased off-diagonal ODF EFT components [12]. This means that frictional steering effects along specific collective adsorbate vibra-tional modes, an effect that LDFA cannot capture, are more likely to be observed at high coverage. In summary, we have applied a machine-learning-based workflow to the simulation of laser-driven H 2 recombina-tion from a Cu(111) surface. The simulation approach enables the study of anisotropic orbital-dependent fric-tion effects and includes all degrees of freedom of the sys-tem. By comparing simulations using ODF and LDFA, we show that the main role of electronic friction lies in mobilising adsorbed hydrogen atoms towards the desorp-tion barrier. While the magnitude of non-adiabatic cou-pling differs substantially between LDFA and ODF, this does not translate into dramatic differences in energy par-titioning of desorbed H 2. As LDFA is known to underes-timate vibrational lifetimes of adsorbed H atoms [15], we expect that overestimation of non-adiabatic coupling for motion perpendicular to the surface leads to an overesti-mation of desorption probability in LDFA. The presented simulation workflow is fully scalable, enabling the future study of steering effects in high coverage scenarios and surface facet dependence of desorption probability. > SI. COMPUTATIONAL DETAILS A. Density functional theory

Density functional theory calculations were performed using the all-electron numerical atomic orbital code FHI-AIMS 1 (version 240920 ) using the SRP48 exchange correlation functional 2 due to its accuracy in describing the H/Cu system. This functional contains a 52 % contribution from the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional 3 and a 48 % contribution from the revised PBE (RPBE) functional 4. SCF convergence criteria were set to 10 -6 eV for total energies, 10 -3 eV for eigenvalue energies, 10 -5 e a 0-3 for charge densities and 10 -4 eV ˚ A-1 for forces. A k-grid of (12 × 12 × 1) was used for all periodic structures. All settings are chosen to be consistent with previous calculations with which the machine learning potentials were constructed 5.Total energies and forces were calculated with the default “tight” basis set with additional “first-tier” basis functions for copper. The electron density for different surface configurations used to train LDFA models was calculated using the output cube total density keyword. To determine the electronic friction tensor (EFT) from first principles electron phonon coupling (orbital dependent friction, ODF), a “light” basis set with additional “second-tier” improvements for hydrogen was found to yield sufficiently converged results. The electronic friction tensor was output using the output friction gamma keyword. All ODF evaluation settings are the same as presented in Refs. 6–8.  

> B. MACE machine learning potential

MACE 9 models were trained by Stark et al using a structure database previously generated to model molecular scattering experiments. Here, we used the models in ref. 5 without further modification as further validation tests confirmed that they are sufficiently accurate to model light-driven recombinative desorption. In the following, we briefly summarise the training data and model hyperparameters defined in previous work 5.The cutoff distance for interactions is 5.0 ˚ A, with a correlation order of 2. Message passing was set to 16 invariant messages, with an interaction order of 2 and no equivariant message passing. Model parameters were optimised over 1500 iterations to minimise errors for forces and total energy with two error weighting schemes. During the first 1200 iterations, force and energy errors were weighted at 95:5. In the final 300 iterations, total energy errors were weighted at 1000:1 over force errors. The training data in ref. 5 contains (3 × 3 × 6) supercells with (100) , (110) , (111) surface facets, as well as (3 × 1 × 6) supercells with (211) surface facets from ab-initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations. These included simula-tions of clean Cu surfaces near thermal equilibrium at 300, 600 and 900 K, as well as simulations with chemisorbed H on Cu at 300 K with the bottom two layers of Cu atoms fixed to prevent unit translations. Further snapshots of molecular scattering processes were included from simulations on (2 × 2 × 4) supercells. Multiple structures with H2 at larger distances to the surface atoms, where interactions are minimal, were included to sample different bond lengths of H 2 in the gas phase. Through adaptive sampling, 1700 additional snapshots from scattering simulations using PaiNN 10 and SchNet 11 models were added to the training database for the MACE model, with a total of 4230 structures.  

> C. Predicting electronic friction tensors

LDFA friction models from ref. 7 were used in this work without further adaptation. These models are based on the Atomic Cluster Expansion using the ACEpotentials.jl 12 Julia package (version 0.6.7 ). The expansion was terminated above a correlation order of 3, with maximum polynomial degrees of 12, 10, and 8. The cutoff distance was set at 4.0 ˚ A. The ACEfriction.jl 6 models used in ref. 7 were used in this work without further adaptation. The atomic cluster expansion was terminated at a correlation order of 2, with 6 th order radial basis functions. A cutoff radius for interactions was specified as 5.0 ˚ A. The learning rate was set to 10 -4 7 .

E. Molecular Dynamics simulations 

All molecular dynamics simulations in this work were performed using NQCDynamics.jl (version 0.15.1 ) and related Julia packages 14 . Some structure manipulations were performed using the atomic simulation environment 15 .

1. Initial conditions 

Initial conditions were generated by Metropolis-Hastings Monte Carlo sampling of atomic positions. All surface structures were generated using a temperature-dependent lattice constant determined from a linear fit for lattice constants measured from NPT dynamics at different temperatures 8. For a unit cell size of 3 × 3 × 6 layers, structures with two adsorbed hydrogen atoms were generated with the atoms located in the hollow adsorption sites. For each temperature, an evenly spaced sample of 100,000 structures was taken from a single random walk, discarding the first 1,000 steps. The initial velocities were generated from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution for the respective temperature. Since the two lowest layers of the Cu(111) surface are frozen, the initial velocities of the Cu atoms in these layers were fixed at zero. MD simulations combining the TTM with electronic friction methods were initialised 0.4 ps prior to the laser pulse maximum with absorbed fluences of 10, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100, and 120 J m -2 . The S4 intensity maximum of the laser pulse corresponds to t = 0. The electronic friction tensor was calculated using one of the two models described in section SI C. Initial positions and velocities corresponding to thermal equilibrium at 100, 150, 200, 250, 300 K were randomly sampled from the distributions described above for each trajectory. TTM-MDEF trajectories were simulated with a time step of 0.1 fs for 5.0 ps total. Simulations are terminated earlier if the centre of mass of the H 2 molecule reaches a distance of more than 6 ˚ A from the surface, indicating desorption. E. Desorption probabilities 

The desorption probability is determined by the number of simulation trajectories in which H 2 desorption was observed compared to the total number of simulations that were performed. A bootstrapping approach is used to resample all trajectories 1000 times to determine a mean and a standard deviation of the probability. Pdes = Ndes 

Ntotal 

H2 molecules are counted as desorbed when the centre of mass of the molecule reaches a distance higher than 6.0 ˚ A from the average position of the top layer of surface slab Cu atoms. Due to the cutoff radii of the ML models, re-adsorption is extremely unlikely at this point. The transition state to desorption is determined based on the mean H-H distance after desorption has begun. Searching backward in time from the point of desorption for at least 20 fs, the onset of desorption is reached when the H-H bond distance increases beyond one standard deviation of the mean (μ + σ).

> SIII. MACHINE LEARNING POTENTIAL VALIDATION FOR LASER-DRIVEN DESORPTION A. MACE potential energy surface

To validate MACE model performance with reference to DFT, we calculate reference energies and forces for a number of structures. While ML model ensemble uncertainty often gives an indication of the error compared to reference data, this correlation becomes less reliable as certain thresholds of accuracy are reached. Since surface diffusion is the most “out-of-dataset” condition for the model previously developed for reactive surface scattering, we pick structures from each desorption trajectory based on the following criteria: 1. 30 evenly spaced structures around and after the transition state for desorption. 2. 30 structures with the highest ensemble energy variance. 3. 30 structures with the lowest ensemble energy variance. 4. 30 structures with the highest summed hydrogen force variance. Comparing energies and forces with reference to DFT for the first three structure sets will enable an assessment of how reliable ensemble uncertainty is as an error metric for simulations on each surface facet, while comparing the first and last data sets should give insight as to whether force variance is indicative of inaccuracy in describing the motion of H atoms.
