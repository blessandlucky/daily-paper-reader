# A Numerical and Experimental Evaluation of Microbubble Communication Using OpenFOAM 

Annika Tjabben ∗, Carolin Conrad ∗, and Hans D. Schotten ∗† ∗German Research Center for Artificial Intelligence (DFKI), Kaiserslautern, Germany 

†Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, RPTU University Kaiserslautern-Landau, Kaiserslautern, Germany E-mail: {Annika.Tjabben, Carolin.Conrad, Hans.Schotten }@dfki.de 

Abstract —Reliable communication in confined environments, such as blood vessels or industrial pipelines, remain challeng-ing due to signal attenuation and limited sensor accessibility. Therefore, this work investigates microbubbles as robust infor-mation carriers within the Internet of Bio-Nano Things (IoBNT) paradigm, leveraging their established use as ultrasound contrast agents. It presents a combined experimental and numerical analysis characterizing microbubble transport under varying flow conditions relevant to biomedical and industrial applications. Experiments with SonoVue microbubbles in a recirculating water channel validate an OpenFOAM-based Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulation using the incompressibleDensePa-rticleFluid solver. Key cases examine water vs. blood-like media and high vs. physiological flow velocities, analyzing the relative influence of fluid properties and advection on microbubble dynamics. Recirculation effects are considered in relation to in vivo circulation timescales. 

Index Terms —6G, Molecular Communication (MC), Mi-crobubbles, Internet of Bio-Nano Things (IoBNT) 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Reliable communication within confined or inaccessible envi-ronments remains a fundamental challenge in both biomedical and industrial contexts. In healthcare applications, conven-tional electromagnetic communication techniques, particularly within the terahertz frequency range, are difficult to apply inside the human body due to strong attenuation and lim-ited penetration depth in biological tissues. Consequently, alternative communication paradigms are required to enable reliable data exchange under such constraints, as envisioned in the emerging field of the Internet of Bio-Nano Things (IoBNT) [1], [2]. In this context, IoBNT conceptual develop-ments and technologies are considered part of next-generation 6G networks and beyond [3]. A promising approach in this context is the use of mi-crobubbles as signaling molecules. Microbubbles are gas-filled particles containing sulfur hexafluoride (SF 6), encapsulated by a phospholipid shell. They are well established in clinical practice as ultrasound contrast agents. There they enhance image quality during diagnostic examinations. Beyond imag-ing, microbubbles have also been investigated for disease

The acoustic properties of microbubbles make them at-tractive candidates for information transfer within biological media. They can be excited and detected using ultrasound waves within specific frequency ranges. Low-frequency ultra-sound can be employed to monitor their motion, whereas high-frequency excitation induces cavitation and bubble collapse, releasing encapsulated substances. This mechanism has been exploited for localized drug or gene release, such as in targeted chemotherapy, minimizing side effects by activating delivery only in affected tissues. Furthermore, recent work has shown that microbubbles can be acoustically steered against fluid flow, enabling controlled propagation even in vascular environments [4]. Building on a previously developed graph-based simulation framework for modeling microbubble propagation in vascular networks, this work advances toward a physics-based Open-FOAM implementation that captures fluid–particle interactions and transport dynamics under realistic flow conditions [5]. This extension enables investigation of microbubble-based communication performance in complex flow environments, bridging theoretical modeling and experimental validation. In addition to biomedical applications, microbubble-based communication can also be extended to industrial scenarios. In liquid-filled or metallic environments, such as pipes in chemical processing plants or cooling systems, traditional electromagnetic communication suffers from severe attenu-ation, while wired sensors require costly installation and maintenance. In such cases, microbubbles introduced into the fluid at specific intervals (e.g., in pulse patterns suitable for On Off Keying (OOK) modulation) can serve as discrete carriers of information. Ultrasonic sensors positioned along the pipeline can detect their presence, frequency, or timing to extract transmitted information. Moreover, analyzing missing or delayed microbubble signals could enable fault detection, such as identifying leaks or flow irregularities. The objective of using microbubbles as information carriers is not to transmit large data volumes but to enable low-rate, ro-bust, and secure signaling in environments where conventional communication channels fail. Such methods could support the transmission of critical control messages or sensitive data, including encryption keys, within otherwise inaccessible areas.

Molecular communication has emerged as a promising paradigm for enabling information exchange in environments where conventional electromagnetic communication is infea-sible, which has lead to extensive research on system models, simulation frameworks, and experimental implementations. 

Farsad et al. provide a comprehensive survey of molecular communication systems, including candidate biological, chem-ical, and physical processes for transmitters, receivers, and propagation mechanisms, and reviewing key system models, modulation and detection techniques, experimental implemen-tations, and open research challenges for future molecular communication networks [6]. To support performance evaluation of diffusion-based links, 

Yilmaz and Chae developed a programmable end-to-end simu-lator for molecular communication via diffusion in one-, two-, and three-dimensional environments [7]. Their framework en-ables the generation of sequential symbol transmissions and in-corporates physical channel properties to analyze Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI), showing that the simulation runtime scales linearly with the number of transmitted signals and that ISI can be mitigated by decision-feedback detection [7]. Building on this work, Yilmaz et al. also derived a closed-form analytical model for a three-dimensional diffusion channel with a fully absorbing spherical receiver, characterizing the time-varying hitting probability and the cumulative number of absorbed molecules as functions of system parameters [8]. These models provide a foundation for understanding signal attenuation and delay in diffusion-dominated molecular channels. Beyond purely analytical and custom-built simulators, general-purpose Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) tools have recently been employed to study particle-based molec-ular communication in more complex geometries. Hofmann et al. employed the Multi-Phase Particle-In-Cell (MPPIC) solver in OpenFOAM to model molecular communication with discrete particles transported in laminar flow, and validated the numerical results against an analytical reference and a MATLAB-based particle simulation [9]. The resulting channel impulse responses were in good agreement, with only minor deviations at larger transmitter–receiver distances. Extending this approach, Zhou et al. introduced a Diffusive Multi-Phase Particle-In-Cell (DMPPIC) solver that augments MPPIC with random walk dynamics to incorporate Brownian motion [10]. In parallel, several specialized reaction-diffusion simula-tors have been developed with molecular communication applications in mind. The Actor-based Communication via Reaction-Diffusion (AcCoRD) framework is a hybrid micro-scopic/mesoscopic simulator implemented in C with MAT-LAB utilities [11]. It allows flexible three-dimensional envi-ronment configurations, includes reactions, flow, and boundary interactions, and is designed to efficiently generate channel statistics such as receiver observation distributions. In contrast, biochemical simulators such as Smoldyn provide highly de-tailed stochastic particle-based reaction-diffusion modeling for cell biology, but are not primarily tailored to communication-system abstraction or metrics [12]. These tools are therefore more suitable when intracellular reaction networks are of interest rather than end-to-end communication performance evaluation. III. T ESTBED

The simulation of the experimental setup was developed using OpenFOAM, an open-source framework for CFD that enables three-dimensional flow simulations. OpenFOAM al-lows the visualization and analysis of complex flow phenom-ena such as turbulence, chemical reactions, and thermal con-duction [15]. The numerical results were visualized, animated, and further analyzed using the open-source software Paraview. The simulation parameters were defined based on the ex-perimental setup, including the choice of solver and time control. Since the simulation involves distilled water and blood as media and requires modeling microbubbles as discrete particles, the solver “incompressibleDenseParticleFluid” was applied. The time control was adjusted to match the duration of a single measurement period. The mesh represents only the most relevant region of the experimental system, specifically,

Due to the deterministic formulation of the underlying model, the numerical results exhibit a high degree of repro-ducibility, with deviations primarily attributable to discretiza-tion and solver configurations. In contrast, the experimental measurements are influenced by noise and environmental fluctuations, leading to minor deviations between individual experiments. Nevertheless, repeated experiments consistently show similar qualitative behavior, indicating the robustness of the observed communication properties. V. R ESULTS AND DISCUSSION 

The first comparison is performed between experimental 1 and numerical case 1, see Fig. 5, which are carried out under identical conditions (water as the medium, flow velocity of 0.351 m/s, and volume flow of 1.5 L/min). This comparison is used to validate the numerical model against the experimental setup. A similar temporal evolution of the microbubble con-centration is expected in both cases, with pronounced peaks shortly after each injection. However, a systematically higher overall concentration is anticipated in the experimental mea-surement. This is attributed to the closed-loop configuration of the experimental system, where microbubbles recirculate and pass the measurement location multiple times, leading to signal accumulation. In the human circulatory system, a similar recirculation occurs: an adult has approximately 5 L of blood volume, with a cardiac output of about 5 L/min at rest, meaning the entire blood volume circulates roughly once per minute [17]. SonoVue microbubbles have a half-life of around 12 minutes in vivo, supporting their persistence for repeated detection. Additionally, the experimental peaks increase over time. This arises from the top-down injection geometry: microbub-bles, being buoyant, tend to accumulate at the top of the syringe containing the NaCl-SonoVue mixture, leading to increasing concentrations with each injection. While this im-proves peak detectability, the numerical model assumes uni-form injection and thus shows constant peak heights. Despite this artifact, the data remain clearly recognizable through es-tablished signal processing techniques. Applying filters such as the Kalman filter or moving-average filter, combined with peak detection algorithms, effectively extract the relevant injection events even from accumulating signals [13]. Peak widths differ slightly—narrower in simulation—which suggests either a shorter effective injection duration or more uniform cross-sectional distribution in the experiment; extending the simu-lated injection period could further improve agreement. The second comparison investigates the influence of the surrounding medium. For this numerical cases 1 (water) and 2 (blood) are compared while keeping all flow parameters constant. The aim is to assess how changes in fluid properties affect microbubble transport, as shown in Fig. 6. Due to the higher density and viscosity of blood, a more damped flow behavior is expected. Consequently, the microbubbles should reach the measurement location with a noticeable temporal delay and potentially reduced peak amplitudes compared to the water case. However, numerical cases 1 (water) and 2 (blood) show nearly identical concentration profiles despite differing fluid properties νwater = 1 .002 ·10 −6 m2/s vs. νblood =3.302 · 10 −6 m2/s. This indicates that microbubble transport is primarily governed by bulk flow velocity (0.351 m/s) rather than medium viscosity or density under these conditions, validating the use of water as a proxy for blood in simulations. The third comparison addresses the impact of flow velocity. Here, numerical case 1 is contrasted with numerical case 3, for which the velocity and volume flow are substantially reduced to approximate arterial conditions, as shown in Fig. 6. At these lower velocities (79 mm/s and below), delayed microbubble ar-rival at the measurement point is expected. The corresponding concentration–time curves should broaden and exhibit tempo-ral stretching. Additionally, the reduced flow speed and lower turbulence intensity influence dispersion, leading to a more smeared-out concentration profile. In contrast, numerical case 3 (arterial-like conditions: 79 mm/s, 0.3393 L/min, Re ≈ 788 )exhibits significantly delayed and broadened peaks relative to case 1. The reduced velocity leads to slower microbubble ad-vection and increased axial dispersion, resulting in a stretched temporal response more akin to physiological flows in smaller vessels. Finally, the simulation of arterial flow conditions (numeri-cal case 3) is compared with the corresponding experimen-tal measurement (exp. 2), both performed at 79 mm/s and 0.3393 L/min; as shown in Fig. 7. A comparable temporal evolution of the concentration signal, characterized by delayed and broadened microbubble detection, is expected in both cases. Agreement between these results further substantiates the consistency of the numerical model with the experimental setup under physiologically inspired flow conditions. The simulation accurately reproduces the delayed peak arrival and broadened profile expected under laminar flow. Experimental concentrations again exceed simulated values due to recirculation, with slightly wider peaks likely reflect-ing experimental dispersion effects (e.g., imperfect injection uniformity or minor wall interactions). Overall, the close qualitative match across flow regimes and between experi-ment/simulation supports the model’s predictive capability for microbubble-based communication in confined, vascular-like environments. VI. C ONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK 

This work presents an OpenFOAM-based simulation frame-work to assess the feasibility of microbubbles as information carriers for reliable communication in confined environments such as blood vessels and industrial pipelines. Experimental measurements using SonoVue microbubbles in a recirculating flow channel, combined with OpenFOAM simulations using the incompressibleDenseParticleFluid solver is used. This pro-vides a characterization of transport dynamics under both laminar and turbulent conditions. The results demonstrate that microbubbles can serve as viable signaling agents within the Internet of Bio-Nano Things framework, offering an energy-efficient alternative to elec-tromagnetic communication in highly attenuating media. The proposed model provides a scalable foundation for designing secure, low-rate communication links in biomedical and in-dustrial systems. Future work will include implementing a fully closed-loop circulatory setup to study the behavior of recirculating microbubbles under realistic flow conditions. The integration of diffusion-based particle solvers will enhance the physical accuracy of nanoscale simulations. Finally, key communica-tion metrics such as the bit error rate, channel capacity, and resilience against eavesdropping will be analyzed to quantify end-to-end system performance. REFERENCES [1] I. F.
